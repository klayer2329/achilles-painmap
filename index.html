ANATOMY

Meet the Achilles tendon

The Achilles is a strong tendon between the calf and heel bone.

01

What it connects

The gastrocnemius and soleus muscles join the Achilles tendon. It connects to the calcaneus, or heel bone.

02

How it moves

It points the foot down and lifts the heel.

03

What it does

It moves force when we walk, run, jump and push off.

FACT

Like a spring

It saves energy and gives it back during push-off.

DESCRIPTION

What is the injury?

Midportion Achilles tendinopathy causes pain and weakness above the heel. Activity often makes it hurt.

WHAT

Not only inflammation

The tendon changes when it cannot handle the amount of activity.

WHO

Who gets it?

Runners and athletes who jump or sprint are affected most. In one large review, the rate was 8% in people over 45 and 2% under age 18. Men and women had similar overall rates.

HOW COMMON

How common?

A review of 16 studies found Achilles tendinopathy in about 6% of active people. In a study of 3,379 runners, 4.2% developed it in 20 weeks and 63.8% of those cases were midportion.

SURPRISE

Images do not show everything

An ultrasound or MRI may look bad even when a person has little pain.

MECHANISM + RISK FACTORS

Why does it happen?

It can happen when activity grows faster than the tendon can adapt.

MORE TRAINING

Too much,
too soon

  • A quick increase in running
  • More hills, sprints or jumps
  • Not enough rest
  • Coming back too fast

LOWER TENDON CAPACITY

SIGNS + SYMPTOMS

What does it feel like?

People often feel the same group of symptoms again and again.

Pain in one place

Usually above the heel in the middle of the tendon.

Morning stiffness

The first few steps may feel stiff or sore.

Pain with activity

Running, jumping or heel raises can hurt.

Warm-up effect

Pain may feel better during activity but return later.

Less endurance

Doing many heel raises becomes harder.

Warning signs

Get help soon for a sudden snap, fast swelling, major weakness, a hot red leg, calf swelling or trouble breathing.

DIAGNOSIS

How is it diagnosed?

A clinician asks questions, checks the leg and may use imaging.

History

When it started, where it hurts, morning stiffness, training changes and past injuries.

Physical exam

The clinician touches the tendon and checks motion, strength, heel raises and hops.

Special tests

Arc sign and Royal London test may help. The Thompson test checks for rupture.

Imaging

Ultrasound or MRI can help in unclear cases, but many people do not need it.

Differential diagnosis means other possible injuries: insertional tendinopathy · Achilles rupture · retrocalcaneal bursitis · calf strain · plantar heel pain
TREATMENT + REHABILITATION

Make the tendon stronger

Change painful activity and slowly add exercise.

PHASE 1

Lower the pain

Do less painful running and jumping. Keep safe activity and start easy calf exercises.

PHASE 2

Build strength

Do more heel raises and slowly add weight with the knee straight and bent.

PHASE 3

Move faster

Add faster calf work, skipping, landing and hopping.

PHASE 4

Return to sport

Slowly add running, sprinting, cutting, jumping and practice.

Surgery: Most people start without surgery. A doctor may talk about surgery if a long and planned rehab program does not help enough.
RETURN TO SPORT

Ready is more than a date

The athlete needs less pain, good strength, good movement and confidence.

✓ Pain is controlled

Daily pain and morning stiffness are not too bad.

✓ Strength is back

The athlete can do strong heel raises many times.

✓ Sport movements feel okay

The athlete can run, hop, land and cut.

✓ Check the next day

Practice does not cause much more pain the next morning.

WALK→JOG→RUN→SPRINT/JUMP→PRACTICE→COMPETE
PREVENTION

Think about the long term

Get the tendon ready for the activity.

1. Add activity slowly

Do not add too much running, hills, sprints or jumps at one time.

2. Stay strong

Train calf strength and endurance during the season.

3. Get enough rest

Put rest between hard workouts and come back slowly after time off.

4. Check your symptoms

Notice morning stiffness and next-day pain.

Interesting fact: The Achilles is the largest and strongest tendon in the body, but it still needs time to adapt.

What this project changed

I chose this injury because I watch the NBA a lot. Many great players had Achilles injuries, especially Kevin Durant and Kobe Bryant. I heard Durant say that when his Achilles broke, he looked behind him because it felt like somebody kicked his calf. This made me want to focus on Achilles injuries.

The most interesting thing I learned is that Achilles injury is not only a sudden rupture. It can also be a chronic problem that slowly gets worse. One athlete story I found was Misty May-Treanor, an American Olympic beach volleyball player. She normally played barefoot on sand, but on Dancing with the Stars she practiced in high heel dance shoes. In 2008, during a jive practice, her Achilles ruptured. She said it felt like she got hit in the back of her leg with a baseball bat. This shows me that a new type of movement, shoes and training load can put a different stress on the tendon.

What surprised me most is that complete rest is not always the best answer for chronic Achilles pain. At first I thought an injured tendon should not be used at all. Now I know that rehabilitation often uses slow and planned calf exercises. The tendon needs the right amount of load so it can become stronger, but too much load too fast can make symptoms worse.

This project changed how I understand Athletic Training. Before, I thought an Athletic Trainer mainly gives ice, tape and helps an athlete after an injury. Now I see they also check the injury, find warning signs, make a rehabilitation plan and decide when the athlete is ready to play again. They need science but they also need to listen to the athlete.
